Street Food in India: A Way of Life on Every Corner

How millions of Indians eat, work, and socialise around food carts and street stalls every single day.

BBC News 17 February 2026 üïê 5 min read
1

Where and When People Eat

Street food is everywhere in Indian cities. In Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore, and Kolkata, food vendors work on street corners, near train stations, and outside colleges. Some vendors have small carts with wheels. Others cook at the same spot every day for many years.

People eat street food at different times. In the morning, workers stop for a quick breakfast before their jobs start. At lunchtime, office workers buy food from stalls near their buildings. The busiest time is evening, between 6pm and 9pm, when everyone finishes work. Late at night, young people meet at food stalls that stay open until 2am or 3am.

Popular locations include railway stations because thousands of people pass through every day. College gates always have food vendors because students want cheap, fast meals. Markets are busy places too. Shoppers take breaks to eat while they buy things. Beach areas attract families who want snacks while they relax.

Most vendors cook outside in the open air. Customers can watch their food being prepared. Many people return to the same vendor for years. They build friendships with the person who cooks their favourite dish. During the rainy season, some vendors work under bridges or inside covered spaces to stay dry.

2

Common Street Food Dishes

Different parts of India have different street foods. In Delhi and North India, people eat chaat ‚Äî savoury snacks with spices. Samosas are very popular: fried pastries filled with potato and peas. Golgappas are small crispy balls filled with spicy water. People eat them in one bite.

Mumbai has its own special foods. Vada pav is called the Indian burger. It is a spiced potato ball inside a bread roll. Another favourite is pav bhaji: mashed vegetables cooked with butter, eaten with soft bread. These foods are quick to eat and filling.

South Indian cities like Bangalore and Chennai serve lighter foods. Dosa is a thin pancake made from rice. It comes with potato filling inside. Idli are soft rice cakes, usually eaten with coconut sauce for breakfast. These dishes are often vegetarian.

In Kolkata, the most famous street food is the kathi roll ‚Äî flatbread wrapped around meat or vegetables. Every region uses local ingredients. Street food tastes completely different as you travel across India. Each city is proud of its special dishes.

3

Why Street Food Matters

"A complete meal from a street stall costs less than one pound ‚Äî making it accessible for millions of workers."

Street food is very cheap. A good snack costs 20 to 50 rupees ‚Äî about 20 to 50 pence. A complete meal from a street stall costs 60 to 100 rupees, less than one pound. Workers with low salaries can afford to eat this food every day. For millions of Indians, street food is the only affordable option.

Many people work as street food vendors. They are self-employed and support their families through this work. A vendor might earn 500 to 800 rupees each day ‚Äî around five to eight pounds. The work is hard. Vendors start very early, sometimes at 5am, and finish late at night.

Starting a street food business does not cost much money. A person needs a cart and some cooking equipment. They do not need bank loans. This helps people from poor backgrounds start their own business. Many vendors learn recipes from their parents and pass the business to their children.

Street vendors buy vegetables at local markets. They buy spices and cooking oil from small shops. This helps farmers and local traders earn money too. Street food creates work for many people, not just the vendors who cook and sell.

4

Social Life and Health Issues

Street food is important for Indian culture and daily life. Food stalls are places where different people meet. A rich businessman and a taxi driver might stand together, eating the same food. Everyone is equal at a food stall. This makes street food special in Indian society.

Many Indians have strong memories connected to street food. They remember eating certain snacks when they were students or children. These foods remind them of home and good times. When Indians live in other countries, they often miss street food very much.

However, street food has health problems. Vendors do not always have clean water to wash dishes and vegetables. Food is cooked outside where there is pollution and dust. Sometimes the cooking equipment is not cleaned properly. This can make people sick, especially tourists who are not used to the food.

City governments find this situation difficult. Street vendors need to earn money and feed people, but dirty conditions create health risks. Some cities try to move vendors to special food areas with better facilities. These plans usually fail because customers prefer the traditional street locations. Despite these problems, street food remains a central part of everyday life in India.
